Maryam in Islam

Maryam bint ‘Imran — Mary, mother of ‘Isa (Jesus) — is a figure of extraordinary rank in the Qur’an. Here is how her story is told.

Named, and named again

Maryam is the only woman named in the entire Qur’an. She is mentioned by name thirty-four times. The 19th surah of the Qur’an is called “Maryam” after her. No other woman, in Islam’s primary scripture, has that distinction.

“And ‘Isa, son of Maryam” — a phrase the Qur’an uses dozens of times, identifying Jesus by his mother’s name alone.

Her birth and dedication

The Qur’an (3:35–37) tells of her mother, the wife of ‘Imran, who vowed to dedicate the child in her womb to the service of the temple, expecting a boy. When a girl was born, she named her Maryam (“she who is elevated”) and prayed for her protection from evil.

Maryam grew up under the guardianship of the prophet Zakariyya, who found that her chamber was provided with sustenance out of season. When he asked her where it came from, she answered: “It is from Allah.”

The Annunciation and the miraculous birth

The Qur’an narrates the angelic visitation (19:16–21), Maryam’s astonishment, and the divine response: “Such is Allah; He creates what He wills; when He decrees a matter He only says to it ‘Be,’ and it is.”

She then withdrew to a remote place. The Qur’an’s account is intimate and human: she cries out in the pain of labour, beneath a palm tree, wishing she had died before this. A voice from below her reassures her, and ripe dates fall into her reach.

The infant’s defence

When Maryam returned to her people carrying the child, they accused her. She was silent, and pointed to her son. And the infant ‘Isa spoke, from the cradle, declaring himself a servant of Allah, a prophet, sent with guidance and blessing — and in the same breath saying: “Peace be upon me the day I was born, and the day I die, and the day I am raised alive” (19:33).

What we share, and where we differ

Muslims and Christians agree on much. We affirm the virginal conception. We affirm Maryam’s purity, her election by God, her dignity. We affirm that ‘Isa is the Messiah, that he spoke as an infant, that he healed the sick, that he will return at the end of time.

We differ on the nature of ‘Isa. Islam holds that he is a prophet and a word from God, but not God incarnate; that he was not crucified, but raised to God, and will return to complete his mission before death.

These differences are real and we do not blur them. But they stand, in our tradition, alongside the affirmation that Maryam herself is chosen above the women of the worlds (Qur’an 3:42). That is common ground worth meeting on.
